## HOMAGE & Statement of Purpose

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> Namo Lokeshvaraya! Though seeing that all phenomena neither come nor go, you strive solely for the benefit of beings. Supreme Guru and Protector Chenrezig, at all times, I humbly pay homage to you with my three doors!
>
> The perfect buddhas—source of all benefit and happiness—arise through accomplishing the sublime Dharma. Since that depends on knowing how to practice, I will explain what bodhisattvas do.

**Pearcey:**
> Namo Lokeśvarāya!
>
> You see that all things are beyond coming and going,
> Yet still you strive solely for the sake of living beings—
> To you, my precious guru inseparable from Lord Avalokita,
> I offer perpetual homage, respectfully, with body, speech and mind.
>
> The perfect buddhas, who are the source of all benefit and joy,
> Come into being through accomplishing the sacred Dharma.
> And since this in turn depends on knowing how to practise,
> I shall now describe the practices of all the buddhas' heirs.

**McLeod:**
> You who see that experience has no coming or going,
> Yet pour your energy solely into helping beings,
> My excellent teachers and Lord All Seeing,
> I humbly and constantly honor with my body, speech, and mind.
>
> The fully awake, the buddhas, source of joy and well-being,
> All come from integrating the noble Way.
> Because integration depends on your knowing how to practice,
> I will explain the practice of all bodhisattvas.

### Commentaries

**HHDL (1974):** The homage goes to Avalokiteshvara because the whole text is grounded in great compassion, of which he is source and embodiment — bowed to through the three doors as inseparable from the guru. The line about seeing that phenomena neither come nor go while still striving for beings announces the union of emptiness-wisdom and compassionate activity that the entire text will teach.

**GR:** The homage frames everything within Avalokiteshvara's compassion; the statement of purpose establishes the bodhisattva path as *learnable* — buddhas emerged from accomplishing the Dharma, accomplishing depends on knowing the practices, so the practices must be taught. "The complete buddhas, source of benefit and ease, have emerged from having accomplished the true Dharma."

**AB:** The gurus are named *before* Avalokiteshvara: without teachers there is no access to any buddha's qualities (he tells the Naropa–Marpa story). "No coming or going" = they see voidness, that nothing exists in impossible self-established ways — and precisely that seeing frees them to work single-pointedly for others. Buddhas were once ordinary confused beings; the stains are "fleeting stains," removable. "The gurus, from their side, aren't going to save us. Rather, by following their instructions… we can gain liberation." [Part 1](https://studybuddhism.com/en/tibetan-buddhism/mind-training/commentaries-on-lojong-texts/commentary-on-37-bodhisattva-practices-dr-berzin/taking-advantage-of-a-precious-human-life)

**KTGR:** Prostration to Chenrezig inseparable from the guru. The point held up: enlightened beings see phenomena as neither coming nor going — empty of inherent existence — *and still* work ceaselessly for beings. That union is the foundational attitude for all 37 practices.

**KS:** The root teacher is honored as inseparable from Avalokiteshvara: perfect wisdom (no coming or going — emptiness beyond fabrication) united with compassion that never rests in a private nirvana. "They have achieved the profound wisdom of emptiness… and become so filled with compassion that their sole desire is to help others."

**TC:** No standalone homage commentary online; her [free commentary book PDF](https://thubtenchodron.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/08/37Practices_of_Bodhisattvas_2018.pdf) covers it. Root text + chanted audio at [thubtenchodron.org](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/04/mahayana-verses/).

**KM:** [Tricycle introduction](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-with-ken-mcleod-introduction/) frames the text as practical guidance usable across traditions; homage material is in [Talk 1 of the 2006 retreat](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-1-2006-37p01/) (background on Tokme Zangpo, translation choices, emptiness and compassion).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** everyone reads the homage as the text in miniature — wisdom (no coming or going) and compassion (striving solely for beings) as one movement, transmitted through a guru. A strong opening theme for session 1: the text will never separate these two.

## VERSE 1 — Precious human life; study, reflect, meditate

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> Having gained this great ship of freedom and fortune—so difficult to find—in order to free yourself and others from the ocean of cyclic existence, listen, reflect, and meditate day and night without distraction. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to study, reflect and meditate,
> Tirelessly, both day and night, without ever straying into idleness,
> In order to free oneself and others from this ocean of saṃsāra,
> Having gained this supreme vessel—a free, well-favoured human life, so difficult to find.

**McLeod:**
> Right now, you have a good boat, fully equipped and available — hard to find.
> To free others and you from the sea of samsara,
> Day and night, fully alert and present,
> Study, reflect, and meditate — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Only "one or two sentient beings in a hundred" obtain the eighteen leisures and fortunes; those actually practicing are the fortunate ones. The body is a ship or tool — discipline it and use it, don't indulge it. And the reach is wide: even people with demanding jobs practice "day and night" by generating the motivation to benefit employers and family — beings who were their past parents — turning work itself into bodhicitta practice.

**HHDL:** The indispensable base is already in hand — human life where Dharma flourishes. Among millions of humans, one equipped with the faculties for practice is rare. "Whether we are young, very young, or old, very old, each of us should make the necessary effort not to waste this precious human life."

**AB:** The "great ship" (echoing Shantideva) is defined precisely: eight respites, ten enrichments. Taking advantage = three activities: *listen* to authentic teachings from reliable sources, *think* until understood, *meditate* — "to meditate actually means to build up a beneficial habit of a new understanding… by repeating it over and over." "Day and night" means consistency applied to real situations and real people, not only formal sessions.

**KTGR:** Not every human body is "precious" — only one with faith in Dharma, analytical wisdom, and diligence. The threefold training in sequence: "First, we listen. Then we use our intelligence to analyze what we have heard—we reflect." Then meditate on the understanding gained.

**KS:** The eight freedoms and ten endowments, with the blind-turtle-and-yoke analogy for rarity. Listening–reflecting–meditating is what turns intellectual understanding into embodied wisdom; without continuous engagement the birth stays meaningless.

**TC:** Frames v1 as the practitioner's mission statement — recall it on waking to counter resistance and lethargy. "That's the reason why you are sitting in the meditation hall all these hours every day. That's your purpose." [Verses 1–3 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/craving-aspiration-different/) · Sangye Khadro adds: real integration shows in hardship — Tibetan monks who endured torture yet stayed grateful for the precious human rebirth. [Our precious human life](https://thubtenchodron.org/2018/12/our-precious-human-life/)

**KM:** "Right now, you have a good boat, fully equipped and available." Commentary in [Talk 1 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-1-2006-37p01/) and [Tricycle verse 1](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-1/) (video, subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the shared move is from *having* the boat to *using* it — and the tension worth discussing: GR says a working parent can fulfill "day and night"; TC/AB emphasize sustained formal training. What does "tirelessly" mean for laypeople in your group?

## VERSE 2 — Leave your homeland

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> Attachment to loved ones stirs you like water. Hatred for enemies blazes like fire. In the darkness of ignorance, you forget what to adopt and what to avoid. Abandon your homeland. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to leave behind one's homeland,
> Where our attachment to family and friends overwhelms us like a torrent,
> While our aversion towards enemies rages inside us like a blazing fire,
> And delusion's darkness obscures what must be adopted and abandoned.

**McLeod:**
> Attraction to those close to you catches you in its currents;
> Aversion to those who oppose you burns inside;
> Indifference that ignores what needs to be done is a black hole.
> Leave your homeland — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Affection for family turns to attachment; harm to loved ones flips attachment into hatred; both root in ignorance. But relocating without transforming the mind does nothing — "abandoning the fatherland" means abandoning the *thoughts* of desire and hatred. The real practice is equanimity: equal compassion for enemies and friends, recognizing even adversaries as past parents.

**HHDL:** Living among family, friends, and enemies keeps the three poisons constantly active. His image: "A home is a place where there is a river of attachment flowing." His caution: the simple monk who leaves home yet becomes attached to his hut, dusting possessions and polishing altar bowls instead of practicing.

**AB:** The homeland runs conditioned habits: attachment "tosses us like water" — "when we're attached to friends who might say, 'Let's go out to the bar and have a drink,' we just follow." Familiar people trigger anger when they break our expectations; naivety obscures what to adopt and abandon. Physical distance (retreat, study program, pilgrimage) gives perspective.

**KTGR:** Attachment builds wave on wave, hatred burns — both from taking people and places as truly existent. Abandoning the homeland principally means seeing "homeland" has no true existence; physical departure is secondary, though advisable if home actually hinders practice.

**KS:** "Native land" = any environment that intensifies the three poisons. He distinguishes literal departure from the deeper relinquishing of the attachments themselves; for laypeople who can't renounce, he prescribes a disciplined schedule balancing work, family, and Dharma.

**TC:** Familiar environments run habitual relational scripts on autopilot — even close relationships as rehearsed performances where each side knows how to trigger the other. "Same old stuff again and again and again. So give up your homeland!" External change supports internal change by removing the stimuli. [Verses 1–3](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/craving-aspiration-different/) · [Sangye Khadro talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/attachment-anger-and-confusion/)

**KM:** Reads "homeland" as the *family system*: "we need to separate from the family system, and that is the essential meaning of 'leave your homeland'" — not severing ties but stepping outside its established patterns, and noticing the discomfort when habitual dynamics break. Note his third poison: not "delusion" but "indifference that ignores what needs to be done is a black hole." [Tricycle verse 2](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-2/) · [Talk 2 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-2-2006-37p02/)

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** a live split worth surfacing in the group — literal vs. internal reading. HHDL/AB/TC lean toward the real usefulness of physical distance; GR/KTGR/KM insist the homeland is a state of mind (family system, reification, desire-hatred). Both agree on the diagnosis: home is where the poisons run on autopilot.

## VERSE 3 — Rely on solitude

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> When negative places are abandoned, disturbing emotions gradually decline. Without distractions, virtuous practice naturally grows. When the mind becomes clear, certainty in the Dharma arises. Live alone in seclusion. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to take to solitary places,
> Avoiding the unwholesome, so that destructive emotions gradually fade away,
> And, in the absence of distraction, virtuous practice naturally gains strength;
> Whilst, with awareness clearly focused, we gain conviction in the teachings.

**McLeod:**
> Don't engage disturbances and reactive emotions gradually fade away;
> Don't engage distractions and spiritual practice naturally grows;
> Keep awareness clear and vivid and confidence in the way arises.
> Rely on silence — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Turns it inward immediately: harmful places are those conducive to hatred, but "the thing that needs to be pacified is not the external environment, but the hatred within our mind." Genuine seclusion = reducing conceptual thinking; even at work, composure and connection to one's true nature achieve the same benefit.

**HHDL:** Solitude supplies the essential conditions — no social involvement, no idle talk, practice "from morning to evening" as all past gurus did. Clear air, few objects of attachment, growing clarity and certainty. Warning: don't become a monastery-dweller consuming offerings while inwardly worldly.

**AB:** Seclusion is the optimal use of the precious human life; cities suit only advanced bodhisattvas. Remove detrimental objects and triggers fade (the ex-partner example); drop the distractions — email, phones, entertainment — and "we have the opportunity to actually face our minds and try to understand what's going on." But seclusion alone guarantees nothing (attachment to your cushion, anger at mosquitoes) — join it with death-and-impermanence reflection.

**KTGR:** Afflictions diminish gross-first, then subtle; clarity gives birth to "certainty and deep trust in the Dharma." Ordinary practitioners should isolate from negativity; accomplished bodhisattvas can stay in negative places and transform them.

**KS:** Outer solitude (remote places) vs. inner solitude (freedom from habitual thought patterns) — inner stillness matters more than physical isolation. Without distraction, virtue, compassion, and bodhicitta flourish; clarity resolves doubts.

**TC:** The antidote-counterpart to v2: "bad objects" aren't intrinsically evil, they're *whatever triggers your own* attachment, anger, confusion. Retreatants verify the verse directly — meditation flows when competing entertainments are absent; the resulting clarity produces conviction through personal verification. [Verses 1–3](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/craving-aspiration-different/) · [Sangye Khadro](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/avoiding-disturbing-distractions/)

**KM:** "Rely on silence." Non-engagement lets reactivity subside on its own; inward listening and looking reveal inherent clarity — "when we listen and look in that clarity, we find a quiet confidence and our way becomes clear." [Tricycle verse 3](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-3/) · [Talk 2 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-2-2006-37p02/)

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the mechanism is agreed — remove triggers, afflictions starve, virtue grows by itself, clarity breeds conviction. The group question is the dose: weekly-sitting-group members aren't hermits. KS's inner/outer solitude distinction and GR's "composure at work" give laypeople a workable version; AB's warning (seclusion guarantees nothing) keeps it honest.

## VERSE 4 — Impermanence; give up this life's concerns

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> Family and longtime companions will part from each other. Possessions gained with effort will be left behind. Consciousness, a guest, will leave the guesthouse of the body. Let go of this life. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to renounce this life's concerns,
> For friends and relatives, long acquainted, must all go their separate ways;
> Wealth and prized possessions, painstakingly acquired, must all be left behind;
> And consciousness, the guest who lodges in the body, must in time depart.

**McLeod:**
> You will separate from long-time friends and relatives;
> You will leave behind the wealth you worked to build up;
> The guest, your consciousness, will move from the inn, your body.
> Forget the conventional concerns — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** "Our body is like a hotel, our mind is like a guest, in the next morning, and the guest has left the hotel." Letting go of this life means releasing *mental* attachment, not dumping your possessions — which would only create difficulties. What we truly need isn't things but loving kindness and compassion; prepare for death by developing bodhicitta, Amitabha visualization, and accumulating merit — the wealth that travels.

**HHDL:** Death strips everyone equally — "the king leaving his kingdom, the beggar his stick." At death companions can't help, relatives can't follow, body and possessions are abandoned in a separation that is permanent. Since the time of death is uncertain, only Dharma provides lasting benefit — prepare now.

**AB:** Practical calibration: the Dalai Lama's 50/50 advice — half our concern for this life's necessary affairs, half for what goes beyond it. Friends part like leaves blown together briefly on karma's winds; possessions "often become garbage. Nobody wants to keep them" — or become inheritance disputes; Shantideva's advice is to give things away *now*. The corpse becomes repulsive — attachment to this temporary form is unrealistic.

**KTGR:** Three facts — loved ones separate, effortful wealth stays behind, the mind is a guest in "a kind of hotel." The renunciation is mental non-clinging, supported by seeing phenomena as dreamlike, lacking true existence.

**KS:** Impermanence meditation dissolves clinging that's rooted in perceiving things as permanent and real. The practice doesn't demand giving everything away; it redirects the energy of clinging toward practice.

**TC:** Enormous mental energy goes into protecting and enhancing exactly these three — relationships, wealth, body — generating karma through attachment, yet all separate from us. Contemplate your own funeral; widen the frame from this lifetime to beginningless rebirths, and anxiety about loss naturally shrinks. [Verses 4–6 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/all-beings-deity/) · [Sangye Khadro on the eight worldly concerns](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/let-go-of-the-eight-worldly-concerns/)

**KM:** "Forget the conventional concerns." Commentary in [Talk 3 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-3-2006-37p03/) (covers vv4–7) and [Tricycle verse 4](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-4/) (subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** every teacher rushes to prevent the misreading — this is not "abandon your family and give away your house" (GR, KTGR, KS are explicit). It's withdrawal of *investment*, calibrated by AB's 50/50. The guest/guesthouse image appears in all of them; it may be the single most quotable line of the preliminaries for your group.

## VERSE 5 — Give up bad friends

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> In bad company, the three poisons increase. The activities of learning, reflecting, and meditating decline, and love and compassion are lost. Give up bad companions. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to avoid destructive friends,
> In whose company the three poisons of the mind grow stronger,
> And we engage less and less in study, reflection and meditation,
> So that love and compassion fade away until they are no more.

**McLeod:**
> With some friends, the three poisons keep growing,
> Study, reflection, and meditation weaken,
> And loving kindness and compassion fall away.
> Give up bad friends — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** The drinking-and-smoking example: you pick up a new friend's habits without noticing. Abandoning such companions requires no fight — "we simply do not agree with their habits and they would naturally stay away from us." The point is protecting bodhicitta from contamination.

**HHDL:** A detrimental friend is one whose company extinguishes love and compassion and erodes study; abandon them "like contagious disease." With right guru and friends one progresses smoothly; with wrong company, good qualities perish.

**AB:** The crucial nuance — "bad friends, or misleading friends, are those who basically, in all good faith, lead us away from our Dharma practice." Not villains: people who ridicule practice or crowd it out, or keep us agitated (his example: the perpetually politics-enraged friend). Matters most for those not yet firmly grounded. Hard cases like marriage: sustain the relationship while maintaining practice; if separation becomes necessary, do it without ill will. [Part 2](https://studybuddhism.com/en/tibetan-buddhism/mind-training/commentaries-on-lojong-texts/commentary-on-37-bodhisattva-practices-dr-berzin/proper-friends-safe-direction-ethics-and-liberation)

**KTGR:** The farmer analogy: a skilled farmer uses even waste productively — an adept can transform poisons into wisdom. Ordinary practitioners can't yet, so for us the association simply degrades virtue. Know which one you are.

**KS:** "Negative friends are much worse because they contaminate our pure mind and destroy our virtuous roots." External conditions shape the inner state, but one's own mind remains the root; group study with virtuous friends is the best protection.

**TC:** "Bad friends don't come with horns on their head… they're people who really mean well" — often our closest relations, who value only this life's money, comfort, reputation, and advise accordingly. Giving them up needs no hostility: stay polite and compassionate, stop *relying on their counsel* — distinguish social courtesy from spiritual reliance. [Verses 4–6](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/all-beings-deity/) · [Sangye Khadro](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/giving-up-bad-friends/)

**KM:** The gardener frame: "when you plant a garden, you take care to create an environment in which the plants you want to grow can grow." Early in practice, deliberately cultivate supportive conditions; apply the verse to your own reactive patterns rather than externalizing blame; build capacity before exposing yourself to difficulty. [Tricycle verse 5](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-5/) · [Talk 3 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-3-2006-37p03/)

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** striking consensus on tone — no aversion, no drama (GR: just stop agreeing; TC: courtesy without reliance; AB: no ill will even in separation). And a shared honesty: the "bad friends" are usually well-meaning people we love. KTGR/KM add the capacity question: protection first, transformation later.

## VERSE 6 — Cherish the spiritual teacher

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> When one follows a true, virtuous friend, faults will diminish and good qualities will grow like the waxing moon. Therefore, cherish this virtuous friend even more than your own body. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to cherish spiritual friends,
> By regarding them as even more precious than one's own body,
> Since they are the ones who will help to rid us of all our faults,
> And make our virtues grow ever greater just like the waxing moon.

**McLeod:**
> With some teachers, your shortcomings fade away and
> Abilities grow like the waxing moon.
> Hold such teachers dear to you,
> Dearer than your own body — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Teachers are like our eyes, giving us a pair "not only to see this world but also the next life." Supplicating with body, speech, and mind generates joy in the teacher, which purifies negative karma and transmits merit; Marpa and Milarepa are the model. A guru's instructions are intangible but precious gifts deserving unlimited gratitude — root gurus, parents, and teachers together.

**HHDL:** Citing Potowa: "to attain buddhahood there is nothing more important than following a guru." But the guru must be *qualified* — learned and experienced in vinaya, sutra, sastra, tantra. Rely as a patient trusts a doctor; maintain pure view.

**AB:** The corrective voice: "entrusting" is examined trust, like choosing a qualified doctor — evaluate actual qualifications, not titles. "We don't worship the teacher either… our disturbing emotions settle down, like muddy water settling down." No attachment or dependency; karmic compatibility matters; the teacher's main gift is *inspiration*, which works even without constant contact. Students should actively build the relationship, not wait for magic. [Part 2](https://studybuddhism.com/en/tibetan-buddhism/mind-training/commentaries-on-lojong-texts/commentary-on-37-bodhisattva-practices-dr-berzin/proper-friends-safe-direction-ethics-and-liberation)

**KTGR:** The definition is *functional*: whoever's reliance makes your defects wane and qualities wax like the moon is your spiritual friend — Naropa with Tilopa, Milarepa with Marpa, hardship included. "You should consider this friend as more precious than your own body."

**KS:** Examine carefully *before* committing; unwavering trust *after*. "Devotion is the major prerequisite for the path; if you have no faith, even Buddha cannot help you." Faith is the vessel through which blessing enters.

**TC:** The teacher's kindness spans lifetimes — "the Buddhas and Bodhisattvas, lifetime after lifetime, hanging in there trying to drag us towards enlightenment!" Real compassion often arrives as direct, uncomfortable feedback, not validation. Proper reliance avoids both fantastical projection and dismissing the teacher as ordinary; it demands releasing attachment to being right. [Verses 4–6](https://thubtenchodron.org/2005/12/all-beings-deity/) · [Sangye Khadro](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/cherish-spiritual-teachers/)

**KM:** Note his translation opens "With *some* teachers…" — same functional test as KTGR. [Tricycle verse 6](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-6/) · [Talk 3 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-3-2006-37p03/)

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the balance point differs by lineage temperament — GR and KS lead with devotion, AB and TC lead with discernment, KTGR/KM with the functional test (watch what actually happens to your faults). Good session material: how does verse 6 sit alongside verse 5? Same criterion, opposite sign — judge companionship by what it does to your mind.

## VERSE 7 — Take refuge in the Three Jewels

### Root text

**Garchen:**
> Who can worldly gods, themselves imprisoned in samsara, protect? Therefore, seek refuge in the Three Jewels, the undeceiving source of refuge. This is the way of a bodhisattva.

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to take refuge in the Three Jewels,
> Since they will never fail to provide protection for all who call upon them,
> For whom are the ordinary gods of this world ever capable of helping,
> As long as they themselves are trapped within saṃsāra's vicious cycle?

**McLeod:**
> Locked up in the prison of their own patterning
> Whom can ordinary gods protect?
> Who can you count on for refuge?
> Go for refuge in the Three Jewels — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Two kinds of refuge, pure and impure. Worldly deities may grant temporary benefits but "are not able to give us the ultimate liberation and lasting benefits." And the pastoral note: when Tara or Chenrezig practitioners still get sick or meet obstacles, that's past karma ripening, not failed practice.

**HHDL:** Worldly gods, imprisoned themselves, can't protect others. "Refuge is an important dividing line, it is this which makes a person a Buddhist or not" — heartfelt acceptance of the Triple Gem, not intellectual knowledge of Buddhism, is what distinguishes practice.

**AB:** Renders refuge as "safe direction" — active, not passive: "we're doing something very active," putting a direction in our life. Precision about the Jewels: the Dharma Jewel is the actual true stoppings and pathway minds; the Sangha Jewel is the aryas with non-conceptual cognition of voidness — not just monastic communities. [Part 2](https://studybuddhism.com/en/tibetan-buddhism/mind-training/commentaries-on-lojong-texts/commentary-on-37-bodhisattva-practices-dr-berzin/proper-friends-safe-direction-ethics-and-liberation)

**KTGR:** "Genuine Dharma is the process of actualizing within our minds the wisdom that realizes emptiness and selflessness." Ultimate refuge is direct realization of emptiness, which self-liberates the afflictions. Brahma can't help; he's a prisoner too.

**KS:** Buddha as teacher, Dharma as path, Sangha as companions — "only the Three Jewels possess the rare and supreme qualities of absolute wisdom and impartial compassion." Three progressive stages of faith: vivid (on encountering), eager (aspiring to embody), confident (from verified understanding). Refuge is conviction, not ritual.

**TC:** "One drowning person trying to save another drowning person. It ain't going to work!" The actual refuge is the Dharma — practical teachings that transform suffering by changing the mind; meditating on the Buddha's qualities generates joy and trust. [Verses 7–9 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2006/01/vajrasattva-enlightened-environment/) · [Sangye Khadro](https://thubtenchodron.org/2019/01/the-gateway-to-the-buddhadharma/)

**KM:** His rendering universalizes it: ordinary gods are "locked up in the prison of their own patterning" — refuge in anything still caught in patterns (people, systems, substances, ideologies) can't hold. [Tricycle verse 7](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-7/) · [Talk 3 (2006)](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-3-2006-37p03/)

**Across the commentaries:** the logic of the verse is identical everywhere — you can't be saved by what's still trapped — but "worldly gods" gets three readings: literal deities (GR, KTGR), the boundary-marker of Buddhist identity (HHDL), and modern refuges-that-fail — wealth, people, patterns (AB, KM). For a mixed group, KM's reading opens the discussion; HHDL's sharpens it. Ties directly to your Ngondro refuge practice.

## Numbering note

All sources here agree on the mapping used above (1 precious human life · 2 homeland · 3 solitude · 4 this life's concerns · 5 bad friends · 6 teacher · 7 refuge). Some editions group or number slightly differently (KTGR's ebook counts 43 verses total, including opening and closing verses) — if a page reference in a book seems off by one, check its scheme.
