## VERSE 12 — When robbed: dedicate everything

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others, in the grips of great desire, should steal,
> Or encourage others to take away, all the wealth that I possess,
> To dedicate to them entirely my body, possessions and all my merits
> From the past, present and future—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if someone, driven by desperate want,
> Steals, or makes someone else steal, everything you own,
> Dedicate to him your body, your wealth, and
> All the good you've ever done or will do — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Thieves are likely karmic creditors — "they may be our past life debtors coming to collect what are truly owed to them." Dedicate merit to them, wish them enlightenment; applied to modern life: forgive uncollectible loans. Anger after loss doubles the damage — even if Amitabha came for you, an angry mind couldn't go. Meeting a thief is reframed as good fortune: debts cleared, generosity practiced.

**HHDL:** Tells the story of the text's own author: robbed near Sakya, Togme Zangpo called "Wait!" — he hadn't had time to dedicate the spoils to them properly — and made a slow, complete dedication. The thieves returned everything and became his disciples. The only verse he illustrates with a biography of the author.

**AB:** A bodhisattva has already mentally offered everything to all beings — "if they were to steal something from us, it's fine as mentally we've already offered it to them." Personal story: children stripped his Dharamsala flower garden; his flash of anger showed him he begrudged others enjoyment while claiming to wish everyone happiness.

**KTGR:** Two reflections make it possible: the thief was your parent in past lives, and dedication with bodhicitta transforms theft into merit for both parties. Milarepa's aunt and uncle stole everything — and that hardship propelled him into Dharma. Candid about difficulty: you cannot practice Dharma while angry; "compassion enables us to be patient so that we can actually dedicate everything."

**KS:** Three reasonings — karmic (loss is the ripening of having deprived others; no one to be angry at but ourselves), compassionate (the thief was our parent; compassion for a harmer purifies obscurations), and the drawbacks of wealth (possessions fetter us between fear and craving; "we should not risk our lives and our virtuous roots for something so insignificant"). Story: the Buddha regarded Devadatta "the same as his only child Rahula" — persecution perfected his generosity and patience.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Tricycle v12](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-12/) reply: "Rather than focus on the suffering they will experience in the future, perhaps consider the suffering they are experiencing now that leads them to inflict pain on others." [Talk 5](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-5-2006-37p05/) applies tonglen to theft.

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the karmic-creditor logic (GR, KTGR, KS) and the already-given logic (AB) are two different engines for the same non-retaliation; HHDL's story shows the verse *lived* rather than argued. Group question: which engine actually works on you when something is taken?

## VERSE 13 — When harmed though innocent: take on their suffering

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others should seek to cut off my head,
> Though I've done them not the slightest wrong,
> To take upon myself, out of compassion,
> All the harms they have amassed—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if you have done nothing wrong at all
> And someone still tries to take your head off,
> Spurred by compassion
> Take all his or her evil into you — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Past-life killing karma ripening; retaliating loses body *and* bodhicitta — "the most important part is that you should never lose your Bodhicitta." Extend compassion to both victim and perpetrator (the killer faces punishment, hatred, and hell — pitiable). Rehearse now: without prior contemplation, "we will immediately take out our gun."

**HHDL:** Brief but direct: even toward one who wishes to kill you while you are innocent, generate strong compassion — "we must practice taking upon ourselves his unskillful deeds, while giving him our merit."

**AB:** Ties it to karmic mechanics: an act's karmic weight depends partly on the victim's suffering, so rage and grudges *heavier* the perpetrator's burden; compassion lightens the outcome for both sides. Sharply distinguished from guilt-based Western forgiveness — it's compassion, not moral bookkeeping.

**KTGR:** The attacker is like a beloved mother gone mad — help, don't punish. Through tonglen absorb the attacker's negativity; the Buddha's former lives show unjust deaths becoming occasions for merit and deepened compassion.

**KS:** Three reasonings: retaliation forges another bad karmic link; separate the person from the affliction (all beings have buddha nature; the harmer has lost self-control and faces terrible ripening); and the deluded "I" is a mere label like calling many trees a "forest" — "our true enemies are not somebody else, but exactly our ego."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 5](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-5-2006-37p05/); [Tricycle v13](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-13/) reflection: how does the fantasy of control indicate a lack of freedom?

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the extreme case is deliberate — every teacher reads it as the stress-test of verse 11's exchange. AB's point that your reaction changes the *perpetrator's* karma is the most distinctive addition; GR's "rehearse before the crisis" is the most practical.

## VERSE 14 — When slandered: praise their qualities

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others should declare before the world
> All manner of unpleasant things about me,
> To speak only of their qualities in return,
> With a mind that's filled with love—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if someone broadcasts to the whole universe
> Slanderous and ugly rumors about you,
> In return, with an open and caring heart,
> Praise his or her abilities — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Slander is like wind — empty — yet reaching the mind it becomes a hurricane; one harsh word can escalate to violence and jail. Triage: if true, it's good medicine; if false, it doesn't matter. His counterintuitive tactic for Dharma-center gossip: publicly declare yourself the maligned person's friend and praise the gossiper — embarrassing the rumor into stopping. "Whatever we hear, no matter whether it is good or bad, it's just like wind."

**HHDL:** Concedes the worldly level — "we should try to establish our innocence" — but the bodhisattva path instead respects and praises the slanderer's qualities. The explicit two-level framing is unusual among these teachers.

**AB:** Counter-criticism destroys the trust that makes helping anyone possible. His example: the Dalai Lama, decades of propaganda against him, refusing to attack in kind and acknowledging positive things China offers — keeping doors open. "Everybody has good qualities, and therefore we rejoice in the happiness that they have from their good qualities."

**KTGR:** The "billion worlds" is now literal — internet-scale circulation. Obscurity serves practice better than fame; "our slanderer is actually a friend who helps deflate our pride, the real enemy."

**KS:** Frames slander through Nagarjuna's eight worldly dharmas — "eight traps" of hope and fear that make the mind fragile; reputation is "an alluring mirage" feeding pride, which blinds wisdom. The slanderer exposes the true enemy and deserves gratitude.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 6, "Friction, Forgiveness, and the Edge of Awakening"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-6-2006-37p06/) (vv14–17): these practices deliberately create friction against habituated patterns to expose attachment to self-image — feel the uncomfortable bodily reactions rather than suppressing them with spiritual jargon. [Tricycle v14](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-14/).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** three teachers independently conclude the slanderer is a *benefactor* (KTGR, KS, GR) via the same mechanism — pride is the real enemy and slander deflates it. KM names what the others assume: the verse is engineered to produce friction you're meant to feel.

## VERSE 15 — When publicly humiliated: see a teacher

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others should expose my hidden faults or deride me
> When speaking amidst great gatherings of many people,
> To conceive of them as spiritual friends and to bow
> Before them in respect—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if someone humiliates you and denounces you
> In front of a crowd of people,
> Think of this person as your teacher
> And humbly honor him — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** At the height of anger, recall bodhicitta and resolve — even at cost of death — not to answer abuse with abuse. Anger is illness, patience the operation; the abuser is "a true and accomplished master" — "it is because of him that we are able to subdue our anger." Notes he himself recited this text daily for 14–15 years and still reflects on it every day.

**HHDL:** Opens with the childhood board-game anecdote (the "yearlong meditator" with a robe over his head), then: we cannot see our own faults, and the Dharma is a mirror — the exposer functions as a great guru. The inversion aphorisms: "Praise is good, but criticism is better"; happiness eats past merit, suffering drives us to Dharma.

**AB:** The critic is a schoolteacher pointing out errors — hiding faults blocks all progress. Model: the Dalai Lama laughing off his own slips — "Thank you. That was a slip of the tongue" — non-defensive acknowledgment as the practice.

**KTGR:** Like a guru who deliberately humbles students to restrain pride; accurate criticism diminishes arrogance, the true adversary. Cites Vairochana and Namkhai Nyingpo accepting exile as beneficial solitude.

**KS:** Suppress the retaliation impulse and you may find the criticism accurate; progress is impossible without courage to face hidden faults — "The best spiritual friend is one who attacks your hidden faults" (Atisha). Closes with the Buddha's four principles: don't return abuse for abuse, anger for anger, exposure for exposure, blows for blows.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Tricycle v15](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-15/) (partially visible): shame arises from violating core values; distinguishes healthy shame from "toxic shame" rooted in childhood conditioning — and notes "to cause shame to another is usually a cause for regret," questioning shaming as a teaching method. [Talk 6](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-6-2006-37p06/).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** Atisha's line (via KS) is the whole verse in one sentence. KM adds the modern psychological caution the traditional commentaries lack — the healthy/toxic shame distinction matters for a Western group; the verse trains *your* response to humiliation, it doesn't license humiliating others.

## VERSE 16 — When betrayed by one you raised: love them more

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others whom I have cared for like children of my own
> Should turn upon me and treat me as an enemy,
> To regard them only with special fondness and affection,
> As a mother would her ailing child—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if a person you have cared for as your own child
> Treats you as his or her worst enemy,
> Lavish him or her with loving attention
> Like a mother caring for her ill child — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** The betrayer is a sick person overwhelmed by karma and afflictions — helpless like a dying patient no doctor can save (citing Padmasambhava). If your compassion doesn't change them it still cannot fail: "Bodhicitta mind is like fire and these happenings are like fire wood." Explicitly extended to breakups and to those attacking the Dharma.

**HHDL:** The possession analogy: a child seized by a spirit attacks his own mother — her task is to separate child from spirit and love him more. "We cannot hate a child or a man with a knife, because they are driven by delusion."

**AB:** Runs on the verse's own parenting logic: a parent doesn't resent the toddler screaming "I hate you" or the sick child crying all night. The betrayer is "sick with some sort of emotional disturbance" — illness calls for more care, not less.

**KTGR:** The mother whose sick child refuses comfort responds with increased love. Story: Sukhasiddhi — beaten and expelled after giving away the family's last rice, and that ingratitude-driven exile made her a great yogini.

**KS:** Cites Chandrakirti's mother of six caring most for the badly-behaved child: "All compassionate lords treat everyone equally, but they especially love and care for bad guys more." The ungrateful can't perceive kindness (no light, no flower seen). Distinctive edge: compassion is not indulgence — it targets persons, not their faults; his academy still expels vow-breakers, and that protects the Dharma rather than abandoning bodhicitta.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv16–21 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 6](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-6-2006-37p06/); [Tricycle v16](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-16/) (the *King Lear* "serpent's tooth" prompt): deal with your own anger first — only then is loving attention authentic.

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the sickness/possession reframe is unanimous — the betrayal is evidence of *their* suffering. KS's boundary (love the person, still enforce the rule) is the crucial nuance for real communities and families; worth raising if the group treats these verses as passivity.

## VERSE 17 — When treated with contempt: honor them as your teacher

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even if others, equal or inferior to me in status,
> Should, out of arrogance, disparage me,
> To honour them, as I would my teacher,
> By bowing down my head before them—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even if your peers or subordinates,
> Put you down to make themselves look better,
> Treat them respectfully as you would your teacher:
> Put them above you — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (His briefest in this stretch.) First examine whether your own showing-off provoked it — the person is "like that of a doctor, coming to cure our sickness" of pride, teaching you that you're not so important. Meet them with gratitude.

**HHDL:** First-person disclosure: "I do this sometimes" — when angered, sit, recite the Eight Verses line on holding oneself lowest, and *visualize bowing to and praising that person*, reversing the machinery that fed hatred. "If you feel shy about this, do it in a corner" — and on the street, pretend nothing's the matter.

**AB:** Shantideva's perspective-exchange: feel how bitter it is to be looked down on from below. The contemptuous person is a guru because "they're teaching us not to be arrogant"; and since enlightenment depends equally on buddhas and sentient beings, all beings merit guru-like respect.

**KTGR:** Contempt is medicine for pride — praise inflates, blame deflates. Distinctive: pride and frustration are mere conceptual constructs, so seeing through them teaches emptiness directly. Story: Rechungpa's scholarly pride, cured by Milarepa's display.

**KS:** We tolerate a boss's contempt but not an inferior's — precisely where the practice bites. Sharp line between patience and passivity: never respond *from anger*, but keep judgment — precept-breakers can't stay in the Sangha ("like pouring a drop of yoghurt into a pot of milk"). Story: H.H. Jigme Phuntsok chanting "Whoever hurts me… I am always glad to accept it."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv16–21 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Tricycle v17](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-17/) framing quote: "We judge ourselves by our motives, and others by their actions." [Talk 6](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-6-2006-37p06/) treats rivalry/pride friction.

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** HHDL's private-visualization practice is the most concrete, immediately usable instruction in the whole adversity block — a ready-made group exercise. KS again supplies the anti-passivity guardrail.

## VERSE 18 — When destitute and ill: take on all beings' suffering

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even though I may be destitute and despised by all,
> Beset with terrible illness and plagued by evil spirits,
> Still to take upon myself all beings' ills and harmful actions,
> Without ever losing heart—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> When you are down and out, held in contempt,
> Desperately ill, and emotionally crazed,
> Don't lose heart. Take into you
> The suffering and negativity of all beings — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Hardship striking a practitioner is old karma ripening *quickly* — like clearing all debts before a long journey — which is why realized masters fall ill or land in jail. The sick should practice tonglen; abandoning Dharma because it "doesn't work" is refusing medicine. Milarepa was poor and ridiculed. "If one wants to know past life causes, just observe your present predicaments." Against spirits: refuge and mantra with confidence — spirits are projections of fear; if your yidam fills the mind's mirror, no spirit can.

**HHDL:** Maps the verse onto the 1974 exile community: lost country, criticism, tuberculosis, the temptation to give up robes — "it is very easy to lose the Dharma." Escalating hypothetical: poor but helped → ill with a peaceful mind → ill with a troubled mind, where practice is most at risk and tonglen most needed.

**AB:** Expansion of perspective: like teenagers discovering others also have alcoholic parents, extending the label "me" to everyone suffering your problem dissolves isolation — "if we expand that beyond just this one individual person, 'me,' to everybody... we will not be discouraged." Voidness prevents the inflated-ego version.

**KTGR:** Story: Gelongma Palmo, whose leprosy drove the Chenrezig practice that cured and realized her; blind reciters of Mani in Tibet. Distinctive point: suffering is a friend that beckons toward Dharma — comfort usually prevents serious practice.

**KS:** Three benefits of suffering: it generates renunciation, destroys arrogance, cultivates compassion — "if we are serious about the Dharma, suffering is preferable to happiness." Discouragement forbidden even for "the most inferior person" (Nagarjuna: "Never be discouraged even when you are as poor as a hungry ghost"). Story: Geshe Potowa's merchants who welcome bad weather — wet hooves travel faster, snow lets them sleep unafraid of thieves.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv16–21 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 7, "Neither High nor Low"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-7-2006-37p07/) (vv18–19): "the overwhelm comes from being separate from, not from being one in it" — tonglen as a way beyond struggle with experience, not a pattern-elimination technique. [Tricycle v18](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-18/).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** this is the verse for the group member going through it. Three distinct consolations: purification-accelerating (GR), suffering-as-teacher (KTGR, KS), and isolation-dissolving (AB, KM). HHDL's version is the most historically moving — he was speaking to refugees about their actual lives.

## VERSE 19 — When prosperous and praised: no conceit

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> Even though I may be famous and revered by all,
> And as rich as Vaiśravaṇa, the god of wealth himself,
> To see the futility of all the glory and riches of this world
> And to remain without conceit—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> Even when you are famous, honored by all,
> And as rich as the god of wealth himself,
> Don't be pompous. Know that the magnificence of existence
> Has no substance — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Only stabilized bodhicitta has substance — "the thing that is useful and can be taken with you is your Bodhicitta." Two faults of attachment to wealth/fame: growing desire obstructs liberation, and it breeds jealousy — schadenfreude at others' misery being a warning sign that one's merit is nearly exhausted.

**HHDL:** The danger point: pride can swell to "I can outdo Nagarjuna"; the proud critic "sitting there like an owl." Cites Dromtönpa: "Though others may rate us very highly, the most expedient course is to see ourselves in the lowest rank" — which he tries to practice. Sociological aside: humility is easy in Tibetan society, hard in the West "because people take advantage of you"; lamas and tulkus especially must practice it.

**AB:** The built-in disadvantages of success: celebrities can't walk down the street, the rich are endlessly solicited — "the more famous we are, the more demands there are on our time... we have no time for ourselves." Use wealth and position for others while they last.

**KTGR:** The wealthy fear poverty, the famous fear obscurity — both suffer while still possessing what they crave. Modern touch: technology accelerates impermanence; today's fame fades tomorrow.

**KS:** Wealth and glory are lightning, dewdrop, bubble, summer rainbow. Antidotes: impermanence meditation, dispelling arrogance ("When arrogance inflates the mind, it draws it down to a state of misery" — Shantideva), and acting as servant of beings — wealth for others' food, clothing, shelter, and ultimately Dharma. Ideal: Potowa's equanimity — no discouragement after ten cities of empty alms bowls, no elation in wealth.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv16–21 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 7](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-7-2006-37p07/): staying present in success as much as in despair. [Tricycle v19](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-19/) questions: what do you feel in your body when you succeed? Has success ever been harder to bear than failure?

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** vv18–19 are a deliberate pair — the two great obstacles, despondency and prosperity (HHDL flags this explicitly at v17's end). KM's Tricycle question ("success harder than failure?") makes the pair immediate for laypeople. HHDL's candor about lamas needing this most gives the verse teeth.

## VERSE 20 — The real enemy: subdue your own anger

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to subdue the mind
> With the forces of loving kindness and compassion.
> For unless the real adversary—my own anger—is defeated,
> Outer enemies, though I may conquer them, will continue to appear.

**McLeod:**
> If you don't subdue the opponent inside, your own anger,
> Although you subdue opponents outside, they just keep coming.
> Muster the forces of loving kindness and compassion
> And subdue your own mind — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Unsubdued inner hatred multiplies outer enemies until the whole village is one; anger primarily wounds the angry ("like your leg had been stabbed"). Love and hatred can't coexist — a chair seats one person. Story: the monk whose nightmares of killing dissolved through bodhicitta — "images generated from Bodhicitta actualize as pureland and images generated from hatred thoughts actualize as hell." Practical vow: don't speak while angry; explain after it subsides.

**HHDL:** Overtly political: quoting Shantideva's leather-soles image, he points to "the Chinese Communists" — from 1959 to "now in 1974" their troubles increase because the enemy within is unconquered. "We have to defeat the enemy within by the force of love and compassion." Applied to mediation: while "them and us" persists, negotiation worsens things; inwardly pacified people become "like new people."

**AB:** The real enemy is internal disturbing emotions; love and compassion are the army. Shantideva: can't cover the earth in leather — cover your feet. Defeating anger once doesn't end the campaign; fittingly, "bodhisattva" in Tibetan means "brave one."

**KTGR:** The Buddha conquered the maras through the samadhi of loving-kindness; Milarepa conquered ego-clinging with selfless wisdom — and his bitter enemies (his aunt!) became devoted disciples. Taming your own mind literally transforms adversaries.

**KS:** The dog chases the stone; the lion attacks the thrower — fools fight circumstances, the wise attack the affliction. "When your mind is tamed, outside enemies cannot hurt you" (Atisha). With bodhicitta, neither the four elements nor humans and non-humans can truly harm you.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv16–21 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 8, "Transforming Anger and Desire"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-8-2006-37p08/) (vv20–22): seven or eight methods for anger across three levels — direct awareness (anger as clarity), energetic, and emotional-component work; "methods don't work uniformly; find what actually works for you." Compassion and reactive anger cannot coexist. [Tricycle v20](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-20/).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** Shantideva's leather-soles image appears in four of them independently — it *is* the verse. KS's lion-and-stone and GR's one-chair are the teachable images; KM's "find what actually works for you" is the honest methodological note a group needs.

## VERSE 21 — Sense pleasures: salt water

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to turn away immediately
> From those things which bring desire and attachment.
> For the pleasures of the senses are just like salty water:
> The more we taste of them, the more our thirst increases.

**McLeod:**
> Sensual pleasures are like salty water:
> The deeper you drink, the thirstier you become.
> Any object that you attach to,
> Right away, let it go — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Identify your strongest desire and analyze in meditation how much suffering it brings, this life and next. Food that satisfies hunger and clothing that warms suffice; at death only the desire-thoughts remain, landing one among hungry ghosts. Candid digression: if students donate but don't practice bodhicitta, "I have failed"; instruction to self-visualize as Avalokiteshvara radiating love while reciting the six syllables. "Worldly desires for pleasure are like a mirage without substance."

**HHDL:** Plain speech: sexual pleasure "appears immediately to us as happiness but deep down it is a cause of suffering," with Nagarjuna's itch: "To scratch an itch is pleasurable but to be without the itch is even better." Structural marker: relative bodhicitta ends here; absolute bodhicitta (vv22–24) begins.

**AB:** Distinguishes longing desire (craving what we lack) from clinging attachment (won't release what we have). Modern examples: the friend hoarding unread Dharma books, parents who can't let grown children go, smartphone and news addiction — "the more we accumulate, the thirstier we get." Abandoning objects is provisional; voidness (v23) is the cure.

**KTGR:** Three lifestyle models: Milarepa abandoned sense objects entirely; Marpa the householder enjoyed them knowing them dreamlike; Gampopa the monk between. True relinquishment is abandoning *attachment* through emptiness, not necessarily contact.

**KS:** "Ants cling to scraps of food, while lions cling to chunks of meat" — desire scales but is identical. Two ways to relinquish: give up the object, or the attachment; beginners may need physical distance until contentment stabilizes. Stories: the Karmapa immediately gave away anything he liked too much; the doctor who could turn any plant, even poison, into medicine — as practitioners should use all situations. "Even though there is rain of precious jewels, greedy people still do not feel content."
