## VERSE 22 — Appearances are mind; mind is beyond fabrication

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is never to entertain concepts,
> Which revolve around dualistic notions of perceiver and perceived,
> In the knowledge that all these appearances are but the mind itself,
> Whilst mind's own nature is forever beyond the limitations of ideas.

**McLeod:**
> Whatever arises in experience is your own mind.
> Mind itself is free of any conceptual limitations.
> Know that and don't generate
> Subject-object fixations — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Good thoughts and bad thoughts are both just thoughts; it's the *body* that needs food and clothing — "as for our mind, it does not need anything." Beauty is his test case: the flower's loveliness exists in the perceiving mind, not the flower; even grief at a parent's death is a thought without substance. Closes by asking the audience directly: drop the judging — wouldn't your suffering disappear?

**HHDL:** His longest philosophical treatment (1974): contrasts Vijnanavada ("all phenomena are of the nature of mind") with Chandrakirti's Madhyamaka — things exist relatively through designation, but analysis finds no base; neither inherently existent nor nonexistent, since they still help and harm. Quotes the Seventh Dalai Lama's songs repeatedly ("I am a yogi of space without birth... a great liar"; "the union of appearance and the void"). Change itself proves emptiness — the spring tree gone bare, the defiled mind able to become omniscient. Describes space-like meditation: rest softly in the moment when the appearance of true existence "suddenly collapses without support" — "Un-seeing is the supreme seeing." Then, disarmingly: "My understanding of these questions is not very good, but I am trying to improve."

**AB:** Voidness = the total absence of impossible modes of existence. His example: perceiving someone as "a horrible person" — the seemingly external horrible object and the perceiving mind arise from the same karmic seed. But the mind is no truly existent projector either — "the mind itself is also devoid of existing as some truly findable entity." Why it matters for bodhicitta: problems aren't fixed in beings from their own side.

**KTGR:** His home ground: outer objects are confused appearances from habitual tendencies, like dream phenomena, while mind has primordially transcended fabrication. Instruction for ordinary practitioners: analyze until *genuine conviction* arises that waking experience is exactly like dream experience — "if we believe otherwise, our desire to cling to a desirable object will increase, and our suffering will grow."

**KS:** An unusually complete map of mind for lay students: perceptual mind (five senses), conceptual mind (we "see" only our thought of the table), emotional mind (seventh consciousness generating self-attachment), and the ocean-like alaya whose seeds project body and world — beings share a common world through common karma while each lives inside his own mind's world. Examining any thought finds no shape, color, or location: empty like a rainbow, yet luminous (even an ant knows pleasure from pain) — clarity and emptiness inseparable, beyond existence and nonexistence. Two supports for realization: systematic study *and* the blessings of lineage masters — without them, "like waiting for sunshine in a north-facing cave." Quote: "O Sariputra, absolute truth is only realized through faith."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv22–24 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** Split across [Talk 8](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-8-2006-37p08/) and [Talk 9, "Dissolving Fixations"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-09-2006-37p09/): all experience arises in mind, not externally; uses Douglas Harding's "headless" perspective and dream-lucidity analogies. His one visible Tricycle line: "There is no escape from your experience—this is your life!" [Tricycle v22](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-22/)

**Across the commentaries:** the philosophical range is real — HHDL explicitly *rejects* the mind-only reading the verse's first line suggests, while KTGR and KS teach it from within Mahamudra/alaya frameworks. For the group, KS's mind-map is the gentlest on-ramp; HHDL's "un-seeing" passage the deepest water. Don't try to resolve the Madhyamaka/Mahamudra difference in one session — display it.

## VERSE 23 — Attractive things: summer rainbows

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to let go of grasping
> When encountering things one finds pleasant or attractive,
> Considering them to be like rainbows in the summer skies—
> Beautiful in appearance, yet in truth devoid of any substance.

**McLeod:**
> When you come across something you enjoy,
> Though beautiful to experience, like a summer rainbow,
> Don't take it as real.
> Let go of attachment — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Brief.) The good-looking bodies of the young turn ugly with age though the person is the same; foods judged delicious or foul "all turn out to be the same" after eating — judgments of the senses are untrustworthy. Strong attachment to pleasant feelings means equally strong loss when they vanish.

**HHDL:** The realization's *purpose*: not to reject appearances but to stop ignorance's exaggeration of objects, which breeds attachment and hatred — "attachment is always backed by ignorance." Cites Aryadeva; distinguishes the old antidote (seeing the object as impure — temporary suppression) from the new (seeing it as unreal — eradication), and advises practicing both.

**AB:** Nothing establishes beauty from the object's own side — like a rainbow, gorgeous at a distance, nothing findable up close. Precision: things are *like* an illusion, not literally illusions — "it's not saying that everything is an illusion" — a distinction with karmic teeth (killing an illusory horse ≠ killing a real one). Even after realizing voidness, automatic attachment persists; repeat the meditation.

**KTGR:** All five sense objects are appearance-emptiness inseparable; understood as empty they "can be enjoyed without attachment, without suffering" — experience becomes relaxed, spacious, affliction-free. Quote: "All forms, sounds, smells, tastes, and tactile objects have no self-nature."

**KS:** Mastery of mind, not avoidance of the world: one who has mastered mind wanders undistracted in a crowd; one who hasn't churns thoughts "like ripples on water" even in retreat. Quote: "It is not what you perceive that binds you, and it is your clinging to it that binds you. Cut through your clinging, Naropa!" (Tilopa).

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv22–24 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 9](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-09-2006-37p09/): grasping pleasant experience is like trying to hold a rainbow — open appreciation instead. [Tricycle v23](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-23/): are you able to open up to not-holding?

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the shared surprise — this verse *permits enjoyment*. KTGR, KS, and KM all land on appreciation-without-grasping rather than avoidance; AB adds the technical guardrail (like-an-illusion, not illusion); HHDL explains why this antidote outclasses the old asceticism. The deep answer to verse 21's provisional one.

## VERSE 24 — Adversity: the death of a child in a dream

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to recognize delusion
> Whenever one is confronted by adversity or misfortune.
> For these sufferings are just like the death of a child in a dream,
> And it's so exhausting to cling to delusory perceptions as real.

**McLeod:**
> All forms of suffering are like dreaming that your child has died.
> Taking confusion as real wears you out.
> When you run into misfortune,
> Look at it as confusion — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Suffering is simply the mind not being calm; triggers differ but the turmoil is one. Grief over a father's death fades within months — proof it's "only a form of thought in your mind." Held as real, pain compounds — he notes it can drive people to suicide. Practical redirection for bereavement: think the person has gone to Amitabha's pure land and recite the mantra.

**HHDL:** (Brief.) Hatred arises from suffering circumstances; seeing that such suffering "lacks real existence" and viewing it as illusion stops hatred — the post-meditation counterpart to v23's treatment of attachment.

**AB:** The dream-child's death is devastating until waking; "terrible" is a socially constructed label, not something in the situation — "taking deceptive appearances to be true is a tiresome waste." His homely case: stubbing your foot hurts by cause and effect, but inflating it into catastrophe adds nothing but pain.

**KTGR:** The grief feels real though the child never existed; taking delusive appearance as true exhausts the mind. Insistence on *repetition*: analyze until conviction is genuine, so when adversity actually strikes, the recognition holds and the bodhisattva remains undisturbed. "All suffering is delusive, like the suffering in a dream."

**KS:** The essential warning: "It is not enough to glibly think, 'Oh, it's only an illusion.'" First analyze *why* appearances are delusive, then familiarize again and again in your own life — only then does awareness hold when difficulty comes.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv22–24 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 9](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-09-2006-37p09/): painful experience recognized as mental construct — like losing possessions in a dream, no damage to your essential nature. His visible Tricycle line: "We can't disown our own experiences, and yet we continually do so." [Tricycle v24](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-24/)

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** vv23–24 are a matched pair — illusion-like view applied to what we want (23) and what we fear (24). The pastoral risk is real and the teachers meet it head-on: KS's "not enough to glibly think it's an illusion" and GR's suicide warning both insist this is trained insight, not bypassing. Handle with care in the group — grief is not to be talked out of, it's to be seen through, slowly.
