## VERSE 25 — Generosity

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to give out of generosity,
> With no hopes of karmic recompense or expectation of reward.
> For if those who seek awakening must give even their own bodies,
> What need is there to mention mere outer objects and possessions?

**McLeod:**
> If those who want to be awake have to give even their bodies,
> What need is there to talk about things that you simply own.
> Be generous, not looking
> For any return or result — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Generosity is what lightens the burden of "I": giving of goods, of Dharma (teaching, mantra recitation for others when one has no money), and of life (ransoming animals from slaughter). Strikingly extended to civic life: pay taxes, utility bills, and fares *gladly* — roads and electricity benefit everyone — curing the stinginess that leads to hell. Work itself becomes generosity of the body: serve your employer as a parent.

**HHDL:** No commentary on the 1974 source for this verse (root verse only).

**AB:** Generosity is fundamentally a *state of mind* — the willingness to give — so even the penniless practice it; but with actual resources, visualized giving isn't enough. Free of all expectation: no payback, no gratitude, not even the karmic ripening. "We give simply because somebody needs something." Humbling self-test: can you let a mosquito have a meal of your blood?

**KTGR:** If bodhisattvas give even bodies — as the Buddha did for the starving tigress — possessions need no mention. Must flow from bodhicitta, not desire to bank merit; includes material giving and the gift of Dharma; removes the root obstacle of clinging.

**KS:** A perfection is "transcendent" when it destroys its counterpart (miserliness), is held by wisdom free of the three concepts, fulfills beings' aspirations, and matures their potential. Three types: material giving (intention matters, not size), protection from fear (saving lives), and Dharma. "The motivation behind the act of giving makes all the difference." Stories: Patrul Rinpoche buying thousands of animals from slaughter; Sodargye's own annual mass animal-release campaigns.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv25–28 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/); his visible Tricycle line: "It is challenging to let go of our attachment to results." [Tricycle v25](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-25/)

**Across the commentaries:** the shared definition is negative — generosity is the *absence of the return-loop*. GR's tax-paying paramita is the most startling lay application in the whole compendium; AB's mosquito test the most honest self-check.

## VERSE 26 — Discipline

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to observe ethical restraint,
> Without the slightest intention of continuing in saṃsāric existence.
> For lacking discipline one will never secure even one's own well-being,
> And so any thought of bringing benefit to others would be absurd.

**McLeod:**
> If you can't tend to your needs because you have no moral discipline,
> Then intending to take care of the needs of others is simply a joke.
> Observe ethical behavior without concern
> For conventional existence — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Discipline spans monastic and lay precepts, national law (a lawbreaker in jail can help no one), Dharma rules, and household non-harm. Extended relationship ethics: examine a partner's *mind* (honesty, belief in cause and effect), vow fidelity. Rules exist "to protect our minds from wavering" — not to measure others. The three bodhisattva moralities, with the reminder to think 100 times daily of benefiting beings.

**HHDL:** Discipline is the main cause of fortunate rebirth — "it is humorous to think of helping others without moral conduct." But morality aimed merely at good rebirth is Dharma without being bodhisattva practice; the correct chain runs: to help beings → buddhahood → practices → precious human body → therefore sila, "without a samsaric aim."

**AB:** Ethical self-discipline restrains destructive behavior and builds constructive strength — distinct from worldly disciplines (athletic training, dieting to look good). "Without worldly intents" excludes practicing to impress teachers or gain status. Togme Zangpo's argument: if discipline is indispensable for your own goals, claiming to serve everyone without it is absurd.

**KTGR:** The three disciplines (refraining, gathering virtue, benefiting beings); "if, without discipline you cannot benefit even yourself... how will you accomplish Buddhahood for all other beings?" Even ethics kept to gain divine or human rebirth is subtle samsaric attachment.

**KS:** Discipline supports all three vehicles "the way oceans and mountains are supported by the underlying mass of the earth"; the three tiers of vows transmute upward "like iron into gold." Anti-pride warning from the Great Jewel Heap Sutra: one who prides himself on pure precepts is the worst breaker. Lay application: vegetarianism must come from inner conviction, not custom or pressure — discipline kept "with joy and intelligence." Quote: "Inferior practitioners break precepts; middling practitioners attach to precepts; superior practitioners do not attach to precepts" (Nagarjuna).

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv25–28 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/) — includes his treatment of the bodhisattva vow vs. individual precepts and how vows are violated at the levels of intention and will. [Tricycle v26](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-26/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the verse's own joke ("simply a joke" / "absurd") is every teacher's anchor — competence before altruism. Nagarjuna's three-practitioners line (via KS) gives the group the full arc: keep the rule, don't worship the rule.

## VERSE 27 — Patience

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to cultivate patience,
> Free from any trace of animosity towards anyone at all,
> Since any potential source of harm is like a priceless treasure
> To the bodhisattva who is eager to enjoy a wealth of virtue.

**McLeod:**
> For bodhisattvas who want to be rich in virtue
> A person who hurts you is a precious treasure.
> Cultivate patience for everyone,
> Completely free of irritation or resentment — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Patience begins with one's own parents — refrain from replying at all. The reward is invisible: the preservation of bodhicitta, guarded life after life. "Our benefactor is actually those who harm us." Then "joyful endurance": bear aching legs, heat and cold (Milarepa's austerities); vow a sleep quota (5–7 hours) and use the saved time for practice. Ultimately, emptiness reveals pain and pleasure equally without substance — the "enlightened joy" beyond both.

**HHDL:** (Brief; opening garbled in source.) The substance: patience toward a *superior* imposing on us "is not the real thing" — real patience is forbearance toward an inferior who harms us when we have full power to retaliate.

**AB:** Patience is not-getting-angry — at harmers, at practice hardships, at the difficulties of helping — and, citing the Dalai Lama, it is strength, not weakness. Distinguish person from act: no anger doesn't mean accepting destructive behavior. "Those with whom we can practice patience are great treasures." Notes the verse's word *habit*: meditation just is building beneficial habits.

**KTGR:** Four qualities of patience (pacifies anger, brings nonconceptual wisdom, generates love, ripens beings). Without harm-doers, patience can't be practiced at all — "whoever or whatever harms us provides the same opportunity for wealth." Story: Milarepa singing to the thieves who attacked him; one became a disciple.

**KS:** Three kinds: bearing harm without anger, enduring hardship for the Dharma with joy, and facing emptiness without fear — so difficult that 5,000 monks walked out when the Buddha taught it. Deepest patience applies emptiness: harm-doer, harmed, and harm all empty — "who is going to get angry at delusions?" Shantideva: "No evil is there similar to anger, no austerity to be compared with patience"; the leather-soles image again.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv25–28 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/) · 2020 [Six Paramitas talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2020/12/six-paramitas/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/). [Tricycle v27](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-27/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** HHDL's criterion cuts deepest — patience only counts when you *could* retaliate. The "harm-doer as treasure" economy (verse's own image) is unpacked identically by AB and KTGR: no adversity, no virtue-wealth. KS's third patience (unafraid of emptiness) links this verse back to 22–24.

## VERSE 28 — Diligence

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to strive with enthusiastic diligence—
> The source of all good qualities—when working for the sake of all who live;
> Seeing that even śrāvakas and pratyekabuddhas, who labour for themselves alone,
> Exert themselves as if urgently trying to extinguish fires upon their heads.

**McLeod:**
> Listeners and solitary buddhas, working only for their own welfare,
> Are seen to practice as if their heads were on fire.
> To help all beings, pour your energy into practice:
> It's the source of all abilities — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** The Mahayana multiplier: reciting 100 manis, visualize *all sentient beings reciting with you* — the merit is as if all beings recited, since no being possesses an "I": "It is like a drop of water, when merged with the ocean, will become the ocean." Recite even on the bus; morning refuge includes all beings.

**HHDL:** No commentary on the 1974 source for this verse (root verse only).

**AB:** "Perseverance" (virya) roots in *hero*: energetic, heroic engagement free of discouragement, laziness, self-deprecation. The a-fortiori argument: if Hinayana practitioners pursuing mere self-liberation would ignore fire on their own heads to keep meditating, how much more a bodhisattva working for everyone. "With this attitude, we endure and go on and on with our spiritual work, regardless of how difficult it is."

**KTGR:** Joyous effort arises naturally from love and compassion, never fear or obligation; diligence carries the same four qualities as patience. Milarepa's visible diligence itself inspired trust in countless beings.

**KS:** True diligence is "joyous effort" — spontaneous, lively, "never grim self-forcing." Three types (armor-like, in virtue, insatiable in serving others), each opposed by a laziness with its antidote: craving comfort → meditate on death; faint-heartedness ("a person like us will never reach enlightenment") → reflect on liberation's benefits; busyness with this life → see it causes only suffering. Diligence permeates all other paramitas.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv25–28 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/) · 2020 [Six Paramitas talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2020/12/six-paramitas/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/). [Tricycle v28](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-28/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** KS's "joyous, never grim" and KTGR's "from love, not obligation" answer the verse's apparent severity — the fire-on-head image sets the *urgency*, not the mood. KS's three lazinesses with antidotes is ready-made group material: everyone recognizes theirs.

## VERSE 29 — Meditative concentration

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to cultivate concentration,
> Which utterly transcends the four formless absorptions,
> In the knowledge that mental afflictions are overcome entirely
> Through penetrating insight suffused with stable calm.

**McLeod:**
> Understanding that emotional reactions are dismantled
> By insight supported by stillness,
> Cultivate meditative stability that passes right by
> The four formless states — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Rest in calm: the ocean's nature is visible only when not churning with waves. His concentration *technique* is contemplative equalization — before an enemy: "this was my parent"; toward beloved children: past-life enemies — until equanimity settles the mind like a cloudless sky. On transcending the formless states: blank-nothingness "emptiness" leads to formless-realm rebirth; the Mahamudra view goes beyond existence and non-existence.

**HHDL:** (Very brief in the 1974 source.) Insight is practiced to realize shunyata and "cut the root of samsara," combined with one-pointed concentration.

**AB:** The verse demands concentration *surpassing* the four formless absorptions — grasping at those blissful states just causes formless-realm rebirth. What's needed is the joined pair: shamatha's settledness fused with vipashyana's perceptiveness; only the union uproots disturbing emotions. His image for the trained mind: a jumbo jet — enormous power, at rest or in flight, fully under control.

**KTGR:** Even the subtlest formless absorption (Peak of Existence) remains samsara; only vipashyana on stable shamatha realizes selflessness and cuts the root. Milarepa's perfected meditation converted hostile scholars.

**KS:** The untrained mind is "a restless monkey"; Shantideva's mad elephant. Distinctive warning: concentrating even on correct-*sounding* thoughts ("everything is created by our own mind," "everything is like space") is still conceptual — seeds for formless realms; the true nature is beyond elaboration, which is why direct pith instructions from a realized master, and verification by one, are required. Prerequisites: seclusion, dropping worldly concerns, death-and-impermanence meditation.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/) · 2020 [Six Paramitas talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2020/12/six-paramitas/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/). [Tricycle v29](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-29/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** unanimous on the core mechanic — calm alone is a cul-de-sac (even blissful formless states); only calm *joined with insight* liberates. KS's warning that even correct-sounding thoughts are still concepts is the subtlest point, and GR's equalization technique turns the whole adversity block (12–21) into a concentration method.

## VERSE 30 — Wisdom

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to cultivate wisdom,
> Beyond the three conceptual spheres, alongside skilful means,
> Since it is not possible to attain the perfect level of awakening
> Through the other five pāramitās alone, in wisdom's absence.

**McLeod:**
> Without wisdom, the five perfections
> Are not enough to attain full awakening.
> Cultivate wisdom, endowed with skill
> And free from the three domains — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Buddhahood is the union of wisdom (Mahamudra) and method (love and compassion); the three spheres are I-the-agent, the method, the goal. Mind neither exists (no form, color, location) nor not-exists (appearances arise from it) — emptiness whose essence contains self-recognizing clarity. Beings' minds equal the Buddha's — rivers becoming one ocean — differing only by admixed afflictions; beings act like lunatics, and "we must look upon their lunatic actions with great compassion" — that compassion is itself the method ripening wisdom into buddhahood.

**HHDL:** No commentary on the 1974 source for this verse (root verse only; his emptiness teaching lives under vv22–24, plus the separate [Deepest Bodhichitta teaching](https://studybuddhism.com/en/tibetan-buddhism/mind-training/commentaries-on-lojong-texts/deepest-bodhichitta-in-37-bodhisattva-practices-the-dalai-lama)).

**AB:** "Wisdom" undersells *discriminating awareness*: decisively distinguishing how things exist from how they appear. Alone it brings liberation; only joined with method — bodhicitta, compassion — does it cut the cognitive obscurations blocking omniscience. "no conceptions about the three spheres": meditator, object, and act all devoid of true establishment; since conceptual cognition projects false solidity, non-conceptual realization is required.

**KTGR:** (A highlight of his commentary.) Prajña realizing twofold selflessness unified with skillful means; the first five paramitas without it cannot bring enlightenment. Contrasts genuine wisdom with false worldly knowledge — denying karma, or science misapplied to harm. Caps it with Milarepa: "There is no other superior knowledge than to realize the true nature."

**KS:** The five perfections without wisdom are "like five blind men who... would never be able to find their way to liberation." Develops through hearing, reflecting, meditating; freedom from the three concepts is the realized state, so the ordinary person's path is unbroken study until buddhahood — "The study of the Dharma will not come to an end until one attains Buddhahood" (Geshe Sharawa). And all wisdom, worldly or transcendent, must be used to benefit beings.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/) · 2020 [Six Paramitas talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2020/12/six-paramitas/).

**KM:** [Talk 10](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-10-2006-37p10/) — perfection operates "free from the three domains," illustrated by a student spontaneously helping a fallen elderly man. [Tricycle v30](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-30/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the three-spheres teaching is the thread — every paramita done *with* a solid giver, act, and recipient stays worldly; done without them, it's transcendent. KS's five blind men and KM's spontaneous-helping story are the two ends of the same point: wisdom is what lets the other five actually see.
