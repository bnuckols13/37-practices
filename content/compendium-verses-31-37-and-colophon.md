## VERSE 31 — Examine your own confusion

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to scrutinize oneself
> Continually and to rid oneself of faults whenever they appear.
> For unless one checks carefully to find one's own confusion,
> One might appear to be practising Dharma, but act against it.

**McLeod:**
> If you don't go into your own confusion,
> You may just be a materialist in practitioner's clothing.
> Constantly go into your own confusion
> And put an end to it — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Brief.) Purely reflexive: "all you need to do is to watch your mind and you will know its appearance" — bad thoughts and arising anger recognized immediately in their appearance. Constant review is the practice.

**HHDL:** No commentary on the 1974 source (its theme is absorbed into his v32 teaching).

**AB:** Only we witness our real motivation — "only we are the best judges of... what is really going on in our minds." The danger is cosmetic spirituality: outward prostrations driven inwardly by guilt, approval-craving, or conformity. The Dharma mirror must face inward, not out at others' failings.

**KTGR:** Signature story: during the scholar Dalo's attack on Milarepa, the disciple Rechungpa grabbed a stick to defend the Dharma — and Milarepa sang him a song on patience, lest the defender violate the very thing defended. Delusion masquerades as virtue in scholar and yogi alike.

**KS:** Practice without self-examination becomes "a facade, a mask." Turn the outward-judging mind inward, especially onto motivation — is ego secretly driving even bodhisattva-like activity? Cites the Sutra Requested by Sagaramati (examining one's own faults + protecting beings = the whole Mahayana) and Gampopa's "hidden faults of practitioners." Quote — H.H. Jigme Phuntsok, near his death: "We should always examine our own mind and be stable and not hurt living beings."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** His translation is the sharpest rendering: "a materialist in practitioner's clothing." [Talk 11](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-11-2006-37p11/): engage confusion directly rather than analyze it intellectually. [Tricycle v31](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-31/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the spiritual-materialism verse. KTGR's Rechungpa story is its perfect illustration — even "defending the Dharma" can be the fault the verse warns about. For a group *leader* (you), this is the verse the whole launch plan already knew: check the seat.

## VERSE 32 — Don't speak of others' faults

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is never to speak ill
> Of others who have embarked upon the greater vehicle,
> For if, under the influence of destructive emotions,
> I speak of other bodhisattvas' failings, it is I who am at fault.

**McLeod:**
> You undermine yourself when you react emotionally and
> Grumble about the imperfections of other bodhisattvas.
> Of the imperfections of those who have entered the Great Way,
> Don't say anything — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Brief.) Never criticize masters, monks, or practitioners — it usually springs from jealousy, and the person hurt is yourself. "We don't really know who is a bodhisattva," so vow never to criticize anyone — and when someone reports another's "evil deeds," don't believe it.

**HHDL:** Faultfinding turned anti-sectarian: the founders of all Tibetan traditions had good reasons; practitioners must not take "them and us" views but train in pure perception. Story: the aged First Dalai Lama, saddened by the prophecy of rebirth in Tushita — "I want to be reborn in a worldly existence in order to go on helping others" — true words of a bodhisattva.

**AB:** The motivational criterion: criticism fueled by jealousy or anger degrades us; respectful, humble, well-timed constructive suggestions can genuinely help. Even a master's sharp correction flows from compassion — intention, not sharpness, is what counts.

**KTGR:** Seven Points of Mind Training: attribute all good qualities to others, all faults to oneself; Patrul Rinpoche's counsel — acknowledge your own deficiency first, then pray for blessings for both parties.

**KS:** "It is hard to tell who is a bodhisattva and who is not" — so criticize no one. Practical: pointing out faults rarely changes people; if it will genuinely help, say it once, pleasantly, and don't repeat it. Deeper ground: pure perception — bodhisattvas may manifest as ordinary people, "wild animals, birds, or dogs." Quote: "An ordinary person cannot assess another ordinary person, only a buddha can."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 11](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-11-2006-37p11/): gossiping about other practitioners' flaws strengthens your own reactivity and delays your own awakening. [Tricycle v32](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-32/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the epistemic humility argument is unanimous — you can't tell who's a bodhisattva, so the safe policy is universal. AB and KS carve out the one exception (single, kind, genuinely helpful correction). Directly relevant to group norms: worth making explicit in session 1's container-setting.

## VERSE 33 — Give up attachment to patrons and gain

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to let go of attachment
> To the households of benefactors and of family and friends,
> Since one's study, reflection and meditation will all diminish
> When one quarrels and competes for honours and rewards.

**McLeod:**
> When you squabble with others about status and rewards,
> You undermine learning, reflection, and meditation.
> Let go of any investment in your family circle
> Or the circle of those who support you — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Brief.) Applies it to sectarian rivalry between Dharma centers: "my teacher is best" is a dispute rooted in fame and gain that shakes listeners' confidence in their own masters. Hearing "this place is good, that place is not" — "we should generate the thought that both are just as good."

**HHDL:** Even very great gurus earned bad reputations through worldly entanglements — his example is Jamyang Shepa and the royal scandal. "Too many worldly friends, patrons, possessions can produce bad consequences"; cites Shantideva's counsel to live at an ordinary level without strong ties.

**AB:** The target is craving for gain and respect. Story: Geshe Ben Gungyal tidying his altar because a patron was coming — then throwing dirt on the offerings when he saw the worldly motive; praised as the best offering in Tibet because it "hit the eight worldly concerns in the face." "We're not doing Dharma practice in order to get other people's approval."

**KTGR:** Desire for gain and honor breeds quarrels and dependency; releasing emotional ties to patrons frees practice from obligations and favoritism.

**KS:** Pursuing gain and honor destroys virtue "like a sword cuts off one's head" (Vinaya); Thogme Zangpo's own words — contentment is the ultimate wealth. Pointed present-day application: distraction by "external rituals, materials, imitation monks or fake tulkus"; monastics free of wealth and fame are "like lotuses in a fire" (Atisha). Dharma starts hard and ends happy; worldly activity the reverse.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 11](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-11-2006-37p11/): emotional investment in groups and family clouds judgment. [Tricycle v33](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-33/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** Ben Gungyal's dirt-on-the-altar (AB) is the definitive story — the eight worldly concerns infiltrate even the shrine. For a brand-new group meeting in a friend's living room, this verse is quietly on point: watch what hosting, status, and belonging do to the practice.

## VERSE 34 — Abandon harsh words

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to avoid harsh words,
> Which others might find unpleasant or distasteful,
> Since abusive language upsets the minds of others,
> And thereby undermines a bodhisattva's conduct.

**McLeod:**
> Abusive language upsets others
> And undermines the ethics of a bodhisattva.
> So, don't upset people or
> Speak abusively — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** The two-sentences-to-a-patient contrast: "hurry up, take your medicine" versus gently saying it will help — same intention, opposite effect. Right motivation needs right method; never speak while angry — "while you are angry you must think the other person is my parent."

**HHDL:** (Brief.) "If one gets into the habit of using harsh speech it is difficult to stop" — the quick-tempered must watch themselves; strike delusion down the moment it arises.

**AB:** Separates malicious intent from forcefulness: shouting to stop a child running into traffic is fine; his own teacher Serkong Rinpoche called him "Idiot" — forceful correction from genuine compassion. What's prohibited is speech *aimed at* hurting.

**KTGR:** (Brief, practical.) Recall the occasions your sharp words caused tears; harsh speech impairs bodhisattva conduct through the very medium of communication — gentle speech guards both parties.

**KS:** Citing Mipham: friendships and conflicts are caused mainly by speech — "most of the wars that devastate the world are started by harsh words." Nagarjuna's typology: words like honey (helpful), like flowers (honest and true), like excrement (violent, false) — discard the third. Gentle speech is itself merit, "the seed of achieving enlightenment."

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 11](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-11-2006-37p11/): harsh speech reinforces one's own confusion while harming others. [Tricycle v34](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-34/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** AB's distinction (forceful ≠ harsh; intention is the criterion) prevents the misreading that bodhisattvas can never speak strongly. Nagarjuna's honey/flowers/excrement typology (via KS) is instantly memorable group material.

## VERSE 35 — Slay afflictions the moment they arise

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to slay attachment
> And the rest—mind's afflictions—at once, the very moment they arise,
> Taking as weapons the remedies held with mindfulness and vigilance.
> For once the kleshas have become familiar, they'll be harder to avert.

**McLeod:**
> When reactive emotions acquire momentum, it's hard to make remedies work.
> A person in attention wields remedies like weapons,
> Crushing reactive emotions such as craving
> As soon as they arise — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Compact.) "Pure motivation and proper methods are like a precious sword and anger is like an enemy." Recognize the thought as bad the instant it appears and it disappears on the spot; indulged a few times, anger accumulates until it erupts.

**HHDL:** (Brief.) Consists of the Eight Verses citation: "In all actions may I watch my mind" — on recognizing delusion, extinguish it from the very beginning.

**AB:** The apparatus: mindfulness is the "mental glue" holding the opponent attitudes, alertness the watchman detecting slippage, attention the re-fixer. Apply specific opponents (love against anger, etc.) *immediately* — like a mispronunciation repeated for years, an entrenched emotional habit becomes nearly impossible to correct.

**KTGR:** Modern analogy: road workers jackhammering protruding rocks — "even out" each affliction at its appearance, before it hardens into habit and matures into action.

**KS:** Every emotion begins as a tiny thought — caught there, it's "like a small wisp of cloud in a clear and empty sky, which is not going to produce any rain"; unnoticed, it multiplies beyond stopping. Mindfulness = remembering what to adopt/abandon; vigilance = constant alertness. Pointed test: "Whatever works to counteract emotions, it is Dharma; whatever does not work is not Dharma" (Dromtönpa) — aimed at practitioners chasing powers instead of counteracting afflictions.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 12](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-12-2006-37p12/): "crushing" reactive emotions means *experiencing them completely* — bodily sensation, emotional content, story — which releases their grip; the opposite of repression. [Tricycle v35](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-35/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** all agree on timing (catch it small — KS's cloud-wisp); the interesting split is on the *verb*. GR/AB/KTGR wield remedies as weapons; KM insists "crushing" means fully experiencing, not suppressing. Both are in the tradition; a rich discussion for practitioners who've tried each and hit its failure mode.

## VERSE 36 — In everything, watch the mind, benefit others

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> In short, no matter what one might be doing,
> By examining always the status of one's mind,
> With continuous mindfulness and alertness,
> To bring about the good of others—this is the practice of all the bodhisattvas.

**McLeod:**
> In short, in everything you do,
> Know what is happening in your mind.
> By being constantly present and aware
> You bring about what helps others — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** In all four activities — walking, staying, sitting, sleeping — watch the mind; "we must daily cleanse from our mind thoughts of hatred and jealousy." Treats the verse as the compressed recap of the whole path's daily conduct: think everyone good, speak well to everyone, treat each person met as your parent.

**HHDL:** Constant mindfulness means "nothing in us is secret, everything is known." Scripts a self-address for the moment a harmful impulse arises — born in the Land of Snows, guided by precious gurus, to act badly with all these opportunities "is deceiving the gurus and bodhisattvas." Ends on his servant theme: be "nothing more than a servant of other sentient beings."

**AB:** The practical summary of the path: continuous self-monitoring in every activity and place. "Mindfulness holds on to discipline, love, compassion, and bodhichitta"; alertness is the alarm. Cites Atisha: watch your speech in company, watch your mind alone.

**KTGR:** The umbrella verse synthesizing all preceding practices — monastery or city, monastic or householder. The examination must reach *motivation itself*: even altruistic deeds checked for hidden pride or subtle self-interest.

**KS:** The homely device: address yourself by name — "Michael, what are you doing now? What is in your mind?" All twelve categories of the Tripitaka reduce to protecting the mind, examining the mind, benefiting others. If intention is purely altruistic, "perfection will naturally arise," wherever you live. Quote: "Everyone wants happiness, but the true way to reach perfect happiness yourself is to bring happiness to others" (Patrul Rinpoche).

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 12](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-12-2006-37p12/): moment-to-moment awareness of inner experience is the *condition* for genuinely helpful action toward others. [Tricycle v36](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-36/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** every teacher reads "in short" literally — this verse *is* the text. Atisha's company/alone rule (AB), KS's name-address device, and HHDL's identity-based self-talk are three concrete forms of the same continuous practice. A natural closing-session verse to send the group home with.

## VERSE 37 — Dedicate the merit

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to dedicate towards enlightenment
> All the virtue to be gained through making effort in these ways,
> With wisdom that is purified entirely of the three conceptual spheres,
> So as to dispel the sufferings of the infinity of beings.

**McLeod:**
> To dispel the suffering of beings without limit,
> With wisdom freed from the three spheres
> Direct all the goodness generated by these efforts
> To awakening — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** (Briefest of all.) All good deeds aim at beings' escape from suffering and enlightenment; dedication via the three-wheel method — purity of agent, act, object — purifies the mindstream: "all sentient beings are Buddhas."

**HHDL:** (Brief.) Dedicate not to one's own well-being or rebirth but "solely to the attainment of buddhahood in order to relieve the suffering of others," conjoined with three-spheres wisdom. "So the main part is now finished."

**AB:** Proper vs. self-centered dedication: "may everybody achieve enlightenment," not "may I be the savior." The caravan metaphor: add your small store of grain to the bodhisattvas' vast stores, all spent for the general good. Three-circles purity prevents grasping at a solid "me" attaining enlightenment.

**KTGR:** The distinctive image: awakening from a dream *while* dedicating — no one dedicates, nothing dedicated, no one receives, all dreamlike. Dedication with this understanding renders merit inexhaustible.

**KS:** Every Dharma activity has three parts — virtuous beginning (bodhicitta), middle (illusory accumulation), end (dedication) — and dedication determines destiny. Merit shared with all beings lasts like "a drop of water that we put into the ocean," while undedicated merit evaporates like a drop on a hot stone and can be destroyed by anger or pride. For ordinary practitioners who haven't realized emptiness: dedicate "in the same way as the past buddhas and bodhisattvas dedicated" — which makes it equally great.

**TC:** Winter Retreat [vv29–37 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 12](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-12-2006-37p12/): dedication done through presence rather than conceptual effort. [Tricycle v37](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-37/) (video/subscriber).

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** KS's ocean-drop vs. hot-stone-drop is the image to close every session with — and his workaround ("dedicate as the buddhas dedicated") solves the practical problem that three-spheres purity is beyond beginners. A dedication verse the group recites weekly would make this verse lived rather than studied.

## COLOPHON — Closing verses

### Root text (Pearcey; McLeod's rendering at [Unfettered Mind](https://unfetteredmind.org/37-practices-of-a-bodhisattva/))

> Here I have set down for those who wish to follow the bodhisattva path,
> Thirty-seven practices to be adopted by all the buddhas' heirs,
> Based on what is taught in the sūtras, tantras and treatises,
> And following the instructions of the great masters of the past.
>
> Since my intellect is only feeble and I have studied but a little,
> This is not a composition likely to delight the connoisseurs,
> Yet since I've relied upon the sūtras and what the saints have taught
> I feel these are indeed the genuine trainings of the buddhas' heirs.
>
> Still, the tremendous waves of activity of the bodhisattvas
> Are difficult for simple-minded folk like me to comprehend,
> And I must therefore beg the indulgence of all the perfect saints
> For any contradictions, irrelevancies or other flaws this may contain.
>
> Through whatever merit has here been gained, may all beings
> Generate sublime bodhicitta, both relative and absolute,
> And through this, come to equal Lord Avalokiteśvara,
> Transcending the extremes of existence and quiescence.
>
> *This was composed in Jewel Cave (Rinchen Puk) in Ngulchu by the monk Tokme, a teacher of scripture and reasoning, for his own and others' benefit.*

### Commentaries

**KTGR:** The humility is Tibetan ritual modesty *and* a safeguard: Thogme disclaims intelligence yet affirms the practices are unmistaken because scripturally based — a paradox guarding against both pride and doubt. The dedication: may all beings become like Chenrezig, transcending both samsara and nirvana's one-sided peace.

**KS:** Five parts: composed from sutras, tantras, shastras, and the Kadampa masters with "no personal invention" — he contrasts modern writers who "write whatever they like... without making reference to sutras and shastras." The self-belittling counteracts pride (Thogme in fact mastered the Five Great Treatises); speaking modestly even when confident "is the behavior of the wise." The final dedication holds the two bodhicittas: relative keeps one from nirvana's extreme, ultimate from samsara's.

**KM:** [Talk 12](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-12-2006-37p12/) covers the closing verses (vv35–41 in his count) — renunciation, presence, and the flow of awakening.

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** Tokme Zangpo's closing move — total confidence in the teaching, zero confidence in himself — is itself the last teaching, verse 31 enacted. A fitting final-session reading: the author models the seat you've been trying to hold all along.
