## VERSE 8 — Lesser capacity: karma and the lower realms

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is never to commit a harmful act,
> Even though not to do so might put one's very life at risk,
> For the Sage himself has taught how negative actions will ripen
> Into the manifold miseries of the lower realms, so difficult to endure.

**McLeod:**
> The suffering in the lower realms is really hard to endure.
> The Sage says it is the result of destructive actions.
> For that reason, even if your life is at risk,
> Don't engage in destructive actions — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Maps each realm to its cause — hatred→hell, stinginess→hungry ghosts, desire→human, pride→god, jealousy→asura, ignorance→animal — with concrete images (Tibetan blizzards, famine) and a warning: those who leap straight to Mahamudra/Dzogchen without contemplating the six realms lose their glimpses of mind's nature the moment afflictions strike. Afflictions must be uprooted at first arising, like a seed before it becomes a tree. "These afflicted emotions are the true enemies that cause us pain and sufferings."

**HHDL:** All lower-realm suffering is karma's production, "whether exactly as described or not" in Abhidharma. He dwells on animal suffering with a near-animal-rights argument — animals harm no one, have no schools, hospitals, or courts, and "actually human beings have no right to eat them." Marks this verse as the end of the smallest-scope path. "All this suffering derives from the unskillful mind."

**AB:** The real teeth of the verse: "constructive behavior is refraining from acting negatively *when we want to act negatively*" — abstaining from what we don't crave is no restraint. On "even at the cost of our lives," he's honest about the difficulty, citing Nazi Germany (refusing service meant execution) and Tibetan monastics who endured torture — a rare attainment worth aspiring to.

**KTGR:** Better to die than commit wrong deeds; suffering is the inevitable consequence of negative actions, created by no god or devil. His distinctive addition: lower-realm suffering consists of confused appearances from habitual tendencies — like suffering in a dream, lacking true existence.

**KS:** Detailed karma mechanics: the four elements of a complete action (basis, intention, execution, completion); each participant in a group killing receives the full result — and symmetrically, each member of a group practice receives the whole group's merit. Emptiness and karma complement rather than contradict: Padmasambhava's "Although my view is higher than the sky, my attention to actions and their effects is finer than flour." Story: Tulku Tisha, an accomplished elder who still begged every master to purify his karma — while modern people feel nothing hearing of hells.

**TC:** Covered in the [verses 7–9 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2006/01/vajrasattva-enlightened-environment/) (Winter Retreat 2005–06).

**KM:** [Talk 4, "Facing Destruction, Finding Freedom"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-4-2006-37p04/) (vv8–10): the point is "not to get rid of the fear—it's to be able to experience the fear but not have to act on it"; harmful action roots in unwillingness to experience discomfort. [Tricycle v8](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-8/): non-reaction robs reactivity of momentum — the stuff often "evaporates into thin air."

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the traditional readings (GR, KS) take the realms as real destinations; KTGR and KM read them as confused appearance and reactive states — and AB quietly bridges by relocating the verse's force into the psychology of restraint under pressure. Good group question: what does this verse ask of you at the moment you *want* to act badly?

## VERSE 9 — Middling capacity: dewdrop pleasures, changeless liberation

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to strive towards the goal,
> Which is the supreme state of changeless, everlasting liberation,
> Since all the happiness of the three realms lasts but a moment,
> And then is quickly gone, just like dewdrops on blades of grass.

**McLeod:**
> The happiness of the three worlds disappears in a moment,
> Like a dewdrop on a blade of grass.
> The highest level of freedom is one that never changes.
> Aim for this — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Behind fleeting happiness much suffering hides; contemplate aging *now* (observe old people so old age won't shock you), and notice how dualistic judgments manufacture likes and dislikes — clothing really just keeps us warm. "The true nature of mind is empty, actually it doesn't need anything."

**HHDL:** A tour of suffering from womb to deathbed — hospitals, oxygen, the failing tongue unable to dictate a will — with the personal note that though disciples would die for him, at death he goes alone: "The king leaving his kingdom, the beggar his stick." Life without Dharma is being a caretaker in one's own house. Distinctive: corrects "many Western books" — nirvana is not the end of existence; one continues, free of delusion.

**AB:** Marks the intermediate scope: liberation from recurring rebirth across all three planes. His plausibility analogy: as other species perceive spectra we can't, minds can experience points on the pleasure–pain spectrum far beyond human physiology. Samsaric pleasures "never last... and they never truly satisfy us"; the exit is the three higher trainings.

**KTGR:** Frames the verse by the three practitioner types — inferior (good rebirth), medium (liberation), supreme (buddhahood for all); happiness anywhere in the three realms is "evanescent as a dewdrop on a blade of grass."

**KS:** Structures it by Mipham's three sufferings — suffering upon suffering, suffering of change ("pleasant when arising, pleasant when remaining, painful when ceasing"), and the all-pervasive suffering of the composite, taught only in Buddhism: conditioned existence as a poisoned meal, a growing tumor, a burning house. With real renunciation, worldly ways feel "like an old man forced to play children's games."

**TC:** Covered in the [verses 7–9 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/2006/01/vajrasattva-enlightened-environment/).

**KM:** [Tricycle v9](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-9/): nirvana reframed as the ongoing process of letting go amid change — "freedom in the experiencing and engaging of life itself." [Talk 4](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-4-2006-37p04/): freedom is recognizing what doesn't change beneath constant change.

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** everyone agrees the dewdrop is the diagnosis; they differ on the prescription's flavor — a *state* to attain (HHDL, KS, AB) vs. a *process* of releasing (KM). HHDL's correction about nirvana ≠ annihilation is worth quoting for any group member carrying that misreading.

## VERSE 10 — Great capacity: our mothers; arousing bodhicitta

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to arouse bodhicitta,
> So as to bring freedom to all sentient beings, infinite in number.
> For how can true happiness ever be found while our mothers,
> Who have cared for us throughout the ages, endure such pain?

**McLeod:**
> If all your mothers, who love you,
> Suffer for time without beginning, how can you be happy?
> To free limitless sentient beings,
> Give rise to awakening mind — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Every being — "a small ant is also our parent" — has been our mother; love must expand to fill space before one can speak of bodhicitta. He divides it into "awareness mind" (eliminating selfishness and the wish for mere self-liberation) and "realized mind" (actually achieving the mind that works for all). Without bodhicitta the 84,000 Dharma methods are useless; with it, all are at hand.

**HHDL:** His longest commentary in this stretch. Seeking liberation alone is "a very wrong and wicked attitude." Universal indebtedness grounded in plain interdependence — the Bodhgaya audience depends on train drivers and electricity workers, "despite the strikes and even the difficulties over petrol" (1974!). Answers the two classic doubts (why strangers? why enemies? — only an enemy can teach patience), then the "noble mind" stories: Atisha's greeting, Dromtönpa's death, Tsongkhapa's hat, plus self-deprecating humor about hot-tempered Amdo men like himself. Admits "I have not completed the path."

**AB:** Precision: bodhicitta is focused on one's own *not-yet-attained* enlightenment, imputable on present causes as a flower is imputable on a seed — built by the seven-part cause-and-effect method (mother-recognition through exceptional resolve). If anyone has been our mother, all equally have. "That is our aim in life, to reach enlightenment to be able to benefit everybody in the most meaningful way."

**KTGR:** Not a being exists who has not been our relation; start with benefactors visualized as mother, extend outward. Honest caution: authentic bodhicitta requires lengthy prior training in love and compassion — he points students to Gampopa's *Jewel Ornament*.

**KS:** Bodhicitta's two aspects — compassion aimed at beings, wisdom aimed at enlightenment; lacking the first you fall into the arhat's nirvana, lacking the second you never exceed ordinary kindness. Story: Atisha at Bodhgaya overhearing two emanations of Tara — the swiftest method is "train yourself in arousing bodhichitta." "Once bodhichitta takes birth in our mind, we are the true children of all buddhas."

**TC:** Covered in the Winter Retreat [verses 10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 4](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-4-2006-37p04/) links awakening mind directly with compassion. [Tricycle v10](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-10/) exercise: picture yourself free of your recurring difficulties — what happens?

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** the mother-recognition argument is universal, but notice the two-sided definitions (KS's compassion+wisdom, GR's awareness+realized, AB's seven-part method) — three different scaffoldings for the same generation of mind. This is the hinge verse of the whole text: everything after it assumes the aspiration is alive.

## VERSE 11 — Exchanging self and other

### Root text

**Pearcey:**
> The practice of all the bodhisattvas is to make a genuine exchange
> Of one's own happiness and well-being for all the sufferings of others.
> Since all misery comes from seeking happiness for oneself alone,
> Whilst perfect buddhahood is born from the wish for others' good.

**McLeod:**
> All suffering comes from wanting your own happiness.
> Complete awakening arises from the intention to help others.
> So, exchange completely your happiness
> For the suffering of others — this is the practice of a bodhisattva.

### Commentaries

**GR:** Self-cherishing has piled up "like non-stop falling snowflakes." His signature method: every time "I" arises — wanting tea, admiring a flower — offer the object first to the Triple Gem; "in every single dollar there is an 'I' attached to it." Tonglen scaled to capacity, from giving to parents up to the Tibetan poor who gave to beggars poorer than themselves. Giving with hope of return yields little merit (citing Milarepa).

**HHDL:** Cites Shantideva's contrast: "Holding oneself dear is the door to every downfall. Holding others dear is the ground of every quality." Gives breath-linked tonglen: take in all beings' suffering on the in-breath, give happiness and merit on the out-breath. Even temporary exchange practice brings peace of mind; self-cherishing yields nothing but worry.

**AB:** The philosophical safety-rail: "me" is only a conventional label, so the scope of care can expand — as the hand helps the foot. Suffering has no true owner; remove it because it hurts, not because it's "mine." "It's only frightening when we think of a solid 'me'" — voidness is what keeps tonglen from becoming a martyr complex.

**KTGR:** Stresses the word *truly* exchange — actual accomplishment, not lip service, possible only after long training on the bodhisattva levels. Detailed tonglen: happiness out as white light, suffering in as black smoke dissolved at the heart. "You should actually be able to accomplish this exchange. Then you are a true Bodhisattva."

**KS:** All fear and struggle arise from clinging to a nonexistent "I" versus "others." Shantideva's graded path: equalize, exchange, cherish others more. First *feel* others' suffering — when an animal is killed, imagine your own head cut off — "otherwise it would just become lip-service." Tonglen per Dilgo Khyentse: begin with your most hated enemy. Story: the master who took a grieving widow's suffering upon himself — her pain vanished instantly.

**TC:** Covered in the Winter Retreat [verses 10–15 talk](https://thubtenchodron.org/series/37-practices-of-bodhisattvas-winter-retreat-2005/).

**KM:** [Talk 5, "Compassion Without Limits"](https://unfetteredmind.org/series-chapter/37-practices-5-2006-37p05/) (vv11–13): distinguishes pain (sensation) from suffering (reaction) — "the problem is not whether you suffer—it's whether you try to avoid suffering." [Tricycle v11](https://tricycle.org/article/37-practices-of-the-bodhisattva-verse-11/) replies: pursuing happiness directly makes relationships transactional; "when you are fully engaged in something, questions of happiness don't arise."

**DKR (your notes):** *…*

**Across the commentaries:** notable convergence on *guardrails* — KTGR (this takes years), AB (emptiness prevents martyrdom), KS (feel first or it's lip-service), KM (don't make it another happiness-strategy). The teachers seem more worried about premature or fake exchange than about reluctance. Strong session pairing with your Ngondro practice: tonglen instructions appear here in four distinct forms.
